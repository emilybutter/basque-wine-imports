The long way around

From Zaragoza to Kansas City

My fascination with the Basque Country began early, during a high-school trip to Spain when I stayed near Zaragoza. It was there that I first learned about Spain's autonomous communities and discovered one in particular whose people spoke a language isolate — Euskara. The Basque language has no known relatives. It is not Indo-European. Linguists have spent two hundred years trying to place it, and have given up.

That initial spark stayed with me through a semester abroad in Granada and later while working in the wine industry in Austin, Texas. With the guidance of an inspiring wine manager and access to an excellent education program, I tasted my first Basque wine — Txakoli — and was, immediately, obsessed.

"There are easier wines to fall in love with. There are not many more interesting ones."

The PhD years

Driven by a growing passion for the language, I pursued and earned a PhD in Basque Studies–Anthropology at the University of Nevada, Reno — one of the few places in North America where you can do serious academic work on the region. My studies let me explore Basque language, culture, and foodways from every angle. I also had the chance to study at a barnetegi — a Basque-language immersion boarding school where students live and learn Euskara around the clock for intensive periods — for three months the year before conducting fieldwork.

It changed everything. Living inside the language — not just studying it — let me understand how it shapes the culture around it. The way Basques talk about wine, food, family, work, the sea: all of it sits on top of a vocabulary that has been around longer than Latin. You can't translate that. You can only point at it.

Coming home

During Covid, I returned to Kansas City, where I was born. It was supposed to be temporary. But the longer I spent here, the more I realized that the city was the perfect place to share the wines I had spent fifteen years getting to know — wines that were largely unknown to American consumers, sold mostly to a small circle of restaurants in New York and the Bay Area, and never to the middle of the country.

I wanted to create opportunities for people here to learn about this culturally rich region the way I had: not just through a tasting note, but through history, language, and tradition. Through the people who made the wines. Through the towns the wines came from.

Topa

In 2025, I partnered with a Basque branding and design company I'd worked with previously to create a logo for what would become Basque Wine Imports — formally, Topa Wine Imports, LLC. The design incorporates the ikurriña — the Basque flag — around the border, and features the Basque word "Topa," meaning "Cheers!"

It's a small thing, but it matters. The wines deserve a label that honors where they come from. So does the language.

Alongside running Basque Wine Imports, I continue to teach as a college adjunct instructor and lead cultural and wine-focused travel experiences for groups from the U.S. Whether I'm in a Kansas City wine bar, a classroom in Reno, a Bilbao cellar, or a pintxos crawl through San Sebastián — I bring a blend of academic insight, enthusiasm, and genuine love for connecting people to the stories, traditions, and gastronomy of the Basque Country.

That's the long version. The short version is: I went to the Basque Country, fell in love, and brought some of it home.

The mark

A word that means cheers

The logo was designed in 2025 by a Basque branding studio I'd worked with previously. The border is the ikurriña — the Basque flag, designed in 1894 by Sabino Arana, and one of the most quietly powerful national symbols in Europe. At the center is the Basque word Topa: a toast, a greeting, a small piece of a language no one else in the world speaks.

The full legal name of the company is Topa Wine Imports, LLC. We do business as Basque Wine Imports — but the spirit of the mark is the toast. Pour something, say Topa, and mean it.

Before you write

A few common questions

Are you taking on new restaurant or retail accounts?

Yes — in Kansas City, St. Louis, and select Midwest markets. We work with a small portfolio and place wines selectively, so a quick conversation about your program is the right first step.

Do you do private tastings?

Often. For private dinners, corporate events, and serious birthdays — I bring four to six bottles, a short presentation, and the story of the place. Pricing depends on group size and travel.

Can I join one of your trips to the Basque Country?

I lead a few small-group cultural and wine-focused trips per year. Sign up for the journal newsletter — that's where new trips are announced first — or write directly and I'll add you to the early-notice list.

Can I buy wine directly from you?

For consumer purchases, ask for us at one of the restaurants and retailers we work with — or reach out and I'll point you to the closest one stocking what you're after.
